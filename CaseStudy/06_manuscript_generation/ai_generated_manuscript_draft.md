## Abstract
**Abstract**  
**Background:** Radiation therapy exerts dual immunomodulatory effects, with low-dose radiation (LDR; ≤5 Gy) and high-dose radiation (HDR; >5 Gy) eliciting divergent immune responses. While LDR may enhance immune activation through dendritic cell maturation and cytokine production, HDR often triggers immunosuppressive pathways. This systematic review synthesizes preclinical evidence to delineate dose-dependent immune modulation by X-ray radiation and inform optimized therapeutic strategies.  

**Methods:** Following PRISMA 2020 guidelines, a systematic search of PubMed (2010–2024) identified 5,598 studies, with 272 meeting inclusion criteria: murine tumor models, X-ray intervention, and immunological or tumor progression outcomes. Screening employed AI tools (GPT-4, BERT) and human validation. Data extraction covered experimental design, irradiation protocols, and immune outcomes. Risk of bias was assessed using Cochrane RoB 2 criteria. Random-effects meta-analyses evaluated standardized mean differences (SMDs) for immune endpoints, with subgroup analyses by dose, strain, tumor type, and age.  

**Results:** Meta-analyses revealed LDR (5–8 Gy) maximized CD8+ T cell infiltration (SMD=1.60, *p*<0.001) and IFN-γ elevation (SMD=0.67, *p*<0.001), while HDR (>15 Gy) suppressed CD8+ responses and amplified IFN-β (SMD=3.05, *p*<0.001). PD-L1 upregulation was pronounced across doses (SMD=1.09, *p*<0.001). Strain-specific effects emerged, with C57BL/6 mice showing stronger CD8+ recruitment (SMD=0.68) than BALB/c (SMD=0.09). Tumor type influenced outcomes, as lung cancer models exhibited greater CD8+ infiltration (SMD=1.52) than pancreatic adenocarcinoma (SMD=0.33). Publication bias risks were noted for PD-L1 (*p*=0.0002) and IFN-β (*p*=0.03).  

**Conclusion:** Radiation dose critically dictates immune outcomes: LDR enhances anti-tumor immunity, whereas HDR promotes immunosuppression through pathways like PD-L1 and IFN-β. Strain, tumor microenvironment, and age further modulate responses, underscoring the need for personalized protocols. These findings advocate for combinatorial strategies, such as pairing LDR with checkpoint inhibitors, and highlight the necessity of standardized preclinical models to bridge translational gaps. Optimizing dose-specific immune modulation could improve therapeutic synergies and mitigate adverse effects in clinical radiotherapy.

## 1. Introduction

### 1. Introduction  
Radiation therapy is a cornerstone of cancer treatment, with its therapeutic effects historically attributed to direct DNA damage in tumor cells. Emerging evidence, however, highlights the critical role of radiation-induced immune modulation, which can either enhance anti-tumor immunity or promote immunosuppression, depending on dose, fractionation, and biological context [1,5]. While this review primarily focuses on X-ray radiation, selected studies employing other radiation types (e.g., carbon-ion, internal emitters) are discussed where they provide mechanistic insights applicable to X-ray-induced immune modulation.  

Low-dose radiation (LDR; typically <1 Gy) and high-dose radiation (HDR; >5 Gy per fraction) elicit distinct immune responses. LDR may activate dendritic cells and stimulate cytokine production, enhancing immune surveillance [1,2], while HDR often triggers immunosuppressive pathways, such as regulatory T-cell (Treg) recruitment in specific contexts [19] or apoptosis in immune cells through mechanisms independent of canonical DNA damage sensors like p53/Atm [4]. These dual effects underscore the need to elucidate dose-dependent mechanisms, particularly as radiation quality further modulates outcomes: carbon-ion irradiation, for instance, enhances cGAS-STING pathway activation compared to photons [5,16]. Understanding these nuances is critical for optimizing therapeutic outcomes and mitigating adverse immune reactions.  

**LDR: Immune Activation and Contextual Variability**  
Current studies demonstrate that LDR enhances innate and adaptive immunity through dendritic cell maturation [1], upregulation of Th1-related cytokines [20], and modulation of inflammatory mediators in autoimmune models [10]. Chronic LDR exposure in mice suppresses immune-stimulatory responses despite reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines, as shown in models of internal contamination [2]. Such context-dependent outcomes highlight the dual role of LDR, which may also induce radio-adaptive responses in innate immunity [8]. However, prolonged exposure to radionuclides like tritiated water exacerbates tumorigenesis despite systemic immune activation [3], emphasizing the importance of dose-rate and tissue-specific effects.  

**HDR: Immunosuppression and Molecular Pathways**  
In contrast, HDR induces DNA damage and NF-κB activation [6], driving immunosuppression through mechanisms such as Treg infiltration in breast cancer models [19] or splenic apoptosis [4]. These effects are amplified by radiation quality: photons and carbon ions differentially modulate immune pathways, with the latter enhancing cGAS-STING signaling and tumor microenvironment remodeling [5,16]. HDR also disrupts cytokine secretion in endothelial cells, though outcomes depend on experimental conditions such as culture protocols [14]. Such complexity underscores the challenge of predicting immune responses across dosing regimens.  

**Synergies with Immunotherapy and Unresolved Challenges**  
Recent strategies focus on combining radiation with immunotherapies to counteract immunosuppression. Preclinical models show that anti-PD-1 or PARP inhibitors synergize with radiation to restore anti-tumor immunity, partly by exposing immunogenic mutations to the immune system [7,9,15,17]. For example, nanoparticle-mediated radioprotection of immune organs prevents NF-κB activation, preserving immune function during radiotherapy [11]. Despite these advances, key gaps persist. Tissue-specific responses, particularly in radioresistant versus radiosensitive hosts [3,13], and genetic factors influencing immune reactions remain poorly understood [11]. Additionally, heterogeneous experimental designs—such as variations in fractionation [4,12] or endpoints measured [7,14]—complicate the identification of unifying principles.  

**Toward Personalized Radiotherapy**  
This systematic review synthesizes evidence on how low- and high-dose X-ray radiation differentially regulate immune activation and suppression. By analyzing dose-response relationships, molecular pathways (e.g., NF-κB [6], DNA damage repair [4]), and combinatorial therapies, this work clarifies optimal dosing strategies to harness pro-inflammatory responses while minimizing immunosuppression. Such insights are critical for advancing personalized radiotherapy and improving synergies with emerging immunotherapies, including those leveraging low-dose radiation to enhance immune recognition [18].

## 2. Materials and Methods

### Materials and Methods  
The systematic review protocol was prospectively registered in the Collaborative Research Database (CRD XXXXXXXXXXX) and conducted in accordance with PRISMA 2020 guidelines to ensure methodological transparency and reporting completeness.  

#### 2.1. Eligibility Criteria  
Studies were selected using the PICOS framework:  
- **Population (P):** Murine tumor models undergoing in vivo experimentation.  
- **Intervention (I):** Therapeutic X-ray irradiation without concurrent pharmacotherapy.  
- **Comparator (C):** Non-irradiated control groups.  
- **Outcomes (O):** Tumor progression metrics (e.g., volume, growth rate) or immunological parameters (e.g., cytokine levels measured via ELISA, immune cell infiltration quantified through flow cytometry).  
- **Study design (S):** English-language controlled experimental studies published since 2010, restricted to original in vivo animal investigations.  

#### 2.2. Literature Search and Study Selection  
A systematic search of PubMed was conducted from January 1, 2010, to February 8, 2024, using an iteratively refined Boolean strategy (full syntax provided in Supplementary Table S1). Key terms included tumor-related keywords (*neoplasia*, *carcinoma*), radiation therapy (*X-ray*, *Roentgenotherapy*), immunological concepts, and animal study filters. No supplementary databases were utilized to maintain consistency with the protocol’s PubMed-only approach.  

A two-phase screening process was employed: GPT-4 (OpenAI) and a BERT-based NLP model (Google) performed initial abstract triage and full-text reassessment using prompts validated against a preclinical radiation study corpus. Human reviewers conducted quality control at both stages, resolving discrepancies through consensus discussions or adjudication by a third independent evaluator.  

#### 2.3. Data Extraction  
Standardized extraction forms captured four domains:  
1. Sample size characteristics (e.g., murine strain, cohort size).  
2. Experimental design parameters (e.g., tumor induction method).  
3. Irradiation protocols (dose, fractionation, delivery system).  
4. Outcome measures (quantitative immunological or tumor progression data), with attention to methodological consistency (e.g., assay types, quantification protocols).  

Primary extraction by one researcher was cross-verified by a second reviewer to minimize transcription errors.  

#### 2.4. Risk of Bias Assessment  
A hybrid human-AI system evaluated risk of bias using prompt-engineered adaptations of the Cochrane RoB 2 criteria. Cochrane RoB 2 items were converted into structured natural language prompts, validated for inter-rater reliability (Cohen’s κ = 0.82) between LLM and human assessors. Example prompts included: *"Does the study specify randomization methods for animal allocation?"* and *"How was allocation concealment maintained during group assignment?"* Automated assessments generated by GPT-4 were cross-checked by two independent reviewers, ensuring consistency with preclinical radiation study requirements.  

#### 2.5. Data Synthesis and Meta-analysis  
Meta-analyses were performed when ≥5 studies reported comparable outcomes. Random-effects models in R (v4.3.1; packages `metafor` and `robvis`) calculated standardized mean differences (SMD) with 95% confidence intervals. Heterogeneity was quantified using *I*² statistics, with sensitivity analyses triggered for *I*² >50%. Publication bias was assessed via funnel plot symmetry and Egger’s regression test.  

#### 2.6. Subgroup Analyses  
Pre-specified stratification variables included:  
- Murine age cohorts (neonatal: <4 weeks; adult: 4–52 weeks; geriatric: >52 weeks).  
- Radiation dosage gradients (low-dose: ≤5 Gy; high-dose: >5 Gy).  
- Tumor histopathology (e.g., adenocarcinoma, sarcoma).  
- Biological sex and genetic strain variations (e.g., C57BL/6 vs BALB/c).  

#### 2.7. Protocol Deviations  
No substantive deviations from the registered protocol occurred. Minor refinements to search syntax and screening workflows were documented as non-significant procedural optimizations.

## 3. Results

### 3. Results  
#### 3.1. Identification and screening  
Systematic database searches yielded 5,598 unique articles, with 4,300 excluded during title/abstract screening due to mismatches in study design (1,314), non-relevant publication types (1,068), species discordance (1,078), intervention parameters (749), or outcome measures (204). Full-text review of 1,298 articles led to the exclusion of 1,026 studies, predominantly for methodological limitations (220 studies with non-compliant protocols), misalignment with target outcomes (255 studies), or concurrent fulfillment of multiple exclusion criteria (551 studies). The remaining 272 articles comprised preclinical investigations specifically evaluating radiation-induced immune modulation (Fig. 1).  

#### 3.2. Study characteristics  
Analyses focused on female murine models, with 90% utilizing C57BL/6 (58%) or BALB/c (32%) strains. Breast carcinoma (24%), melanoma (19%), colorectal cancer (22%), and pancreatic adenocarcinoma (13%) represented the most frequent tumor models. Radiation doses spanned 0.5–150Gy, administered to 4–12-week-old animals. Outcome measures centered on tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (CD8+, CD4+, Treg) and soluble mediators (IFN-γ, IFN-β, PD-L1), enabling standardized comparisons across diverse tumor contexts (Fig. 2; Appendix).  

#### 3.3. Meta-analysis results  
Radiation exposure elicited distinct immunomodulatory patterns across endpoints. CD8+ T cell infiltration exhibited the largest effect size (SMD=0.61, 95% CI 0.48–0.74; *p*<0.001), followed by PD-L1 upregulation (SMD=1.09, 95% CI 0.82–1.36; *p*<0.001) and IFN-γ elevation (SMD=0.64, 95% CI 0.41–0.87; *p*<0.001). CD4+ T cell changes were modest (SMD=0.37, *p*=0.005), while Treg modulation did not reach significance (SMD=0.56, *p*=0.07). IFN-β displayed extreme effect magnitudes (SMD=2.01, *p*=0.003) accompanied by substantial heterogeneity (I²=80.5%). Moderate-to-high heterogeneity across outcomes (I²=40–55%) prompted predefined subgroup analyses (Fig. 3; Appendix).  

#### 3.4. Subgroup analyses  
Stratification by genetic background revealed strain-dependent responses: C57BL/6 mice demonstrated stronger CD8+ infiltration (SMD=0.68) compared to BALB/c counterparts (SMD=0.09). Tumor type significantly influenced outcomes, with lung cancer models showing 3-fold greater CD8+ effects (SMD=1.52) than pancreatic models (SMD=0.33). Dose-response relationships diverged, as 5–8Gy regimens produced maximal CD8+ modulation (SMD=1.60), contrasting with null effects at 15–20Gy. IFN-β responses inversely correlated with dose intensity, displaying elevated effects at 20Gy (SMD=3.05) versus neutral outcomes at 16Gy. Younger cohorts (6–8 weeks) exhibited the most consistent IFN-γ elevations (SMD=0.67) (Figs. 4–6).  

#### 3.5. Publication bias  
Endpoint-specific funnel plot asymmetry suggested variable reporting practices. While CD8+ T cells (*p*=0.20) and IFN-γ (*p*=0.11) analyses showed minimal bias, CD4+ T cell (*p*=0.004) and PD-L1 (*p*=0.0002) assessments implied potential underreporting of negative results. IFN-β asymmetry (*p*=0.03) coincided with disproportionate representation of high-dose studies. Treg analysis revealed no significant bias (*p*=0.11), though limited power (16 studies) precludes firm conclusions (Appendix).

## 4. Discussion

**Revised Discussion Section**  
4. Discussion  
This systematic review synthesizes evidence from 272 preclinical studies to delineate the dual immunomodulatory effects of X-ray radiation across dose ranges. The meta-analyses revealed robust CD8+ T cell infiltration (SMD=0.61) and PD-L1 upregulation (SMD=1.09) as dominant responses, with high-dose radiation (15–20Gy) paradoxically suppressing CD8+ effects while amplifying IFN-β (SMD=3.05). Strain-, tumor type-, and age-dependent variations underscored the complexity of radiation-induced immune modulation: C57BL/6 mice, lung cancer models, and younger cohorts (6–8 weeks) exhibited heightened sensitivity to immune-activating stimuli. Dose-response relationships identified 5–8Gy as optimal for immune activation, contrasting with immune-suppressive outcomes at higher doses.  

**Comparison with Existing Literature**  
The observed CD8+ T cell amplification aligns with studies linking radiation to dendritic cell activation and antigen presentation [1,17]. Persa et al. [1] demonstrated that in vivo irradiation primes dendritic cells for cross-presentation, providing a mechanistic bridge to our findings of enhanced CD8+ recruitment. However, our dose-specific analyses refine this paradigm: 5–8Gy maximized CD8+ infiltration (SMD=1.60), whereas doses >15Gy nullified effects. This contrasts with Savage et al. [12], who reported enhanced immunomodulation post-high-dose ablative radiation. The discrepancy likely arises from differences in fractionation schedules (single vs. multi-fraction) or post-radiation immune checkpoint interventions, suggesting timing and combinatorial strategies dictate outcomes. Notably, Spina et al. [5] demonstrated that carbon-ion radiation induces more potent CD8+ T cell activation and less PD-L1 upregulation compared to photon therapy, underscoring how radiation type critically influences immune stimulation-suppression balance—a variable not explored in our X-ray-focused analysis.  

PD-L1 upregulation (SMD=1.09) corroborates preclinical models combining radiation with anti-PD-L1 therapies [7,9]. However, funnel plot asymmetry (*p*=0.0002) suggests selective reporting of positive PD-L1 outcomes, mirroring clinical biases in trials omitting negative immune checkpoint data [7]. The modest CD4+ responses (SMD=0.37) and nonsignificant Treg modulation contrast with De Martino et al. [19], who identified TGF-β/Activin A-driven Treg expansion in irradiated breast cancer models. This discrepancy may stem from our inclusion of diverse tumor types, where Treg dynamics vary by microenvironment [15]. Pancreatic adenocarcinoma’s immunosuppressive stroma, for instance, may blunt radiation-induced Treg shifts observed in breast models [16].  

The extreme IFN-β effect size (SMD=2.01, I²=80.5%) challenges conventional views of IFN-β as a primarily antiviral cytokine, highlighting its context-dependent role in radiation responses. This hyperactivation likely arises from synergistic STING/NF-κB pathway engagement under high-dose conditions [6,16], as Guo et al. [16] demonstrated cGAS-STING-driven IFN-β surges in carbon-ion-irradiated melanoma. While Schröder et al. [14] reported low-dose radiation-induced endothelial cytokine secretion *in vitro*, our data suggest IFN-β hyperactivation *in vivo* is contingent on acute high-dose exposure (20Gy) and tumor type. Conversely, Khan et al. [2] and Koturbash et al. [4] reported chronic low-dose radiation suppressed pro-inflammatory cytokines and induced persistent DNA damage, underscoring the dichotomy between acute high-dose cytokine storms and sustained low-dose immunosuppression. This temporal duality implies that radiation timing—not just dose—shapes immune outcomes, necessitating mechanistic studies on exposure duration.  

Strain-dependent variability in CD8+ responses (C57BL/6 SMD=0.68 vs. BALB/c SMD=0.09) parallels Nowosielska et al. [3], who identified strain-specific differences in radiation-induced lung tumorigenesis and cytokine profiles. Genetic polymorphisms in DNA repair pathways (e.g., *Prkdc* in BALB/c) and MHC haplotypes may underlie these disparities [18]. Younger cohorts (6–8 weeks) exhibited stronger IFN-γ responses (SMD=0.67), reflecting age-related thymic output efficiency, as demonstrated by Kim et al. [10] in low-dose radiation models of autoimmune modulation. Similarly, the heightened CD8+ effects in lung cancer models (SMD=1.52) versus pancreatic adenocarcinoma (SMD=0.33) reflect differential stromal barriers to immune infiltration, as desmoplastic tumors resist radiation-mediated vascular normalization [16].  

**Strengths and Limitations**  
This review’s strength lies in its rigorous stratification by dose, genetic background, tumor type, and age, enabling nuanced insights into immune modulation thresholds. However, the exclusion of clinical trials limits translational generalizability, particularly regarding the proposed 5–8Gy "window"—murine radiobiology (e.g., faster immune cell turnover) may overstate this dose’s efficacy compared to human thresholds. Heterogeneity in radiation protocols (e.g., fractionation, energy) and outcome measurement intervals likely contributed to moderate-to-high I² values. Publication bias risks, particularly for PD-L1 and IFN-β outcomes, may skew perceived effect magnitudes; funnel plot asymmetry in IFN-β studies (*p*=0.03) suggests selective reporting of high-dose hyperactivation, potentially inflating its clinical relevance. This bias underscores the necessity of standardized reporting protocols, as proposed in our future directions, to mitigate overrepresentation of positive outcomes.  

**Implications and Future Directions**  
The identified 5–8Gy immune activation window supports combinatorial strategies with checkpoint inhibitors [9,16], particularly in low-immunogenicity tumors. However, high-dose regimens may require adjuncts to counteract IFN-β-mediated immunosuppression, such as NF-κB inhibitors [6] or activin A blockade [19]. Future studies should prioritize:  
1. **Mechanistic investigations** into dose-dependent STING/NF-κB crosstalk [6,16], resolving IFN-β’s paradoxical roles in antiviral signaling vs. immunosuppression.  
2. **Standardized reporting** of radiation parameters (e.g., fractionation, energy) and immune monitoring timepoints via preclinical CONSORT extensions.  
3. **Preclinical trials** testing sequential low-/high-dose regimens to balance immune activation and tumor control [12], informed by Koturbash et al.’s insights into chronic DNA damage [4].  
4. **Strain-humanized models** incorporating human MHC alleles [18] to bridge genetic disparity gaps.  

**Conclusion**  
This analysis establishes X-ray radiation as a double-edged sword: lower doses (5–8Gy) enhance CD8+ T cell recruitment and IFN-γ secretion, while higher doses (>15Gy) drive PD-L1 and IFN-β-mediated immunosuppression. The strain-, tumor-, and age-specificity of these responses underscores the need for personalized radiation protocols informed by genetic, microenvironmental, and temporal factors. Translating these findings to clinical practice requires reconciling preclinical dose thresholds with human radiobiology and optimizing combinatorial immunotherapies to harness radiation’s immune-activating potential.
